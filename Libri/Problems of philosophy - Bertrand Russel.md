
# Chapter 2 -  The Existence of Matter

The idea is that we want to know what we can know for certain, this way we can build upon this base of knowledge. 
To carry out the reasoning Russel at first uses the principle of doubt (like Descartes) and tries to find something he cannot doubt.

The first thing to consider is whether we can say an external reality with real external matter separate from our sense-data exists. 
But we cannot for sure prove that an external reality exists, after all it could be a dream in a coma. This case would be undistinguishable from the one where real matter exists as we only have access to sense-data to perceive the supposed external objects.
To prove external real public matter exists we cannot use the fact that multiple people seem to experience the same sense-data because in the dream/coma case we also have no way of proving other people are real and not just a result of our imagination, this would be just affirming the conclusion by assuming other people exists.

After this Russel explains how even if we have no definitive proof that external matter exists, even if we would like to, we also have no real reason to doubt it and it's a simpler explanation than conjuring the coma example.
It also provides explanatory power like the cat example he gives:
If I see a cat in some room and later in the adjacent room, assuming external reality exists can allow me to reach the conclusion the cat moved from the previous to the current position through some set of intermediate positions and allows to predict stuff about the world (he makes an example about the cat being hungry as time has passed even if I was not sensing the cat in between)
 
The general message to get from this example is that we want to build a hierarchy of instincts from which we get our beliefs, and the only way we disprove a belief is by having some other contradiction with another held belief.
We could still be wrong about these instincts (all of them or part of them) but we have no choice in this regard

The general message of this example is that our beliefs are based on certain instinctive beliefs that we cannot fully prove with certainty.
Philosophy tries to organize these beliefs into the a coherent system when beliefs conflict we reject the ones that conflict the most or explain our experience less. Even though these instinctive beliefs could still be wrong (e.g. no external reality), we have no choice but to start reasoning from them.
